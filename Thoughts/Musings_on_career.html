Musings on career

What makes a founders foundry?

A lot of people who end up running an exciting project of their own have learned at some institution, typically a company.
What common features do these companies have?
  1. High talent bar.
  2. Ensure carefully that they are working on the right problem.
  3. Lots of ownership allowing for independent thinking.
  4. Technology leaders.
  5. Allow you to network with relevant players.
  6. Flat hirachies, no bureaucracy.
  7. Exposure to a lot of problems.
  8. Strong networks.
  9. Fast paced.

Companies

Some companies might add a unique spin on the top points.
  1. Palantir
    • No sales people. Engineers go directly to the customer, to really find out how the software is used and deployed.
    • Some articles on what makes Palantir special: The unconventional Palantir principles that catalyzed a generation of startups | The founder foundry
  2. Google
    • 20% time rule: Engineers get to spend 20% of their time on whatever they think is most benefitial for Google. This produced google news, Gmail and even AdSense!
  3. Stripe
  4. OpenAI
  5. Google Deepmind
  6. Nvidia?
  7. Just running your own
It seems noteworthy that all the firms listed above are large, really large. Do they produce so many successful founders because size comes with some innate qualities, or is it simply the law of large numbers?.
After some thinking I assume that they have the qualities listed above and the law of large numbers. Any firm that fulfills the above criteria should be a mini founders foundry.

Consulting

When thinking about the Palantirs and Googles, one must think about the BCGs and McKinseys and Bains, too. They produced a lot of successful founders, they produced a lot of successful executives. When I look at my parent's generation, many of those whom I find inspiring did consulting in the past. Alas, I ask myself:

  1. What is it that made consulting such formidable school in the past? Is it the points listed above?
  2. Is consulting still a formidable school in the present?
  3. The world has changed. Is consulting still relevant? If yes, how did it adapt? If no, what did it miss?
