Biographies

General thoughts

I really like reading Biographies. First, I got into history when I read "Diplomacy" from Henry Kissinger. This made me realize just how little I know about the history and workings of China, so I read a Ezra Vogel's biography on Deng Xiaoping. What a relevation! Gone are the drab days in school where I am forced to recount the dates of some supposedly relevant events.
Biographies allowed me to learn a lot about history, following the path of those who shaped it. Biographies automatically make it a story, for you follow someones life. I assume that it gives you a somewhat distorted view of History, overplaying the influence of individuals in one's mind. But then again, some distortion is unavoidable anyways, and the biography makes history so enjoyable, that I am happy to make this trade.
Also, Biographies let you learn from others.
My favourite biographies are on Churchill (Andrew Roberts), Deng Xiaoping (Ezra Vogel) and Duveen (Beermann).
Walter Isaacson's books are also really good. I liked his book on Steve Jobs and even got to meet him at an event at Harvard. I also heard excellent things about Moses (Robert Caro).

Example: Churchill

I loved Churchill for going and seeing for himself. One incredible example of the importance of going and seing for oneself is the fall of singapore in the second world war. Initially he was not concerned with Singapore, for it was widely considered to be an impenetrable fortress. When the Japanese were in a position to attack, He asked for the status of the sigaporian fortifications and recieved the following telegram:

Until quite recently all plans were based on repulsing seaborne attacks on island and holding land attack in Johore [located in the southern portion of the Malay Peninsula] or farther north, and little or nothing was done to construct defences on north side of the island to prevent crossing Johore Straits, though arrangements have been made to blow up the causeway.

- General Wavell, Commander-in-Chief of ABDACOM (American-British-Dutch-Australian Command)
The japanese had realized this too and, after invaded Malaya first, took singapore from the north. Churchill greatly regretted his mistake.
I do not write this in any way to excuse myself. I ought to have known. My advisers ought to have known and I ought to have been told, and I ought to have asked. The reason I had not asked about this matter, amid the thousands of questions I put, was that the possibility of Singapore having no landward defences no more entered into my mind than that of a battleship being launched without a bottom.

- Winston Churchill, "The Hinge of Fate"
The Prime Minister should not risk his life unnecessarily…. Was there ever such a good target as the one presented by our not inconspicuous Prime Minister perched up high on a Jeep? Nobody could have mistaken or missed that massive figure, complete with cigar to identify him….
- Captain Alec Cunningham-Reid, in Parliament
